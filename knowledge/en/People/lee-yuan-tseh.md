# Lee Yuan-tseh

Lee Yuan-tseh represents a name that illuminated Taiwan on the world's scientific stage. Born in Hsinchu in 1936, he became Taiwan's first Nobel Prize laureate through his passion for science and relentless research spirit. His invention of "crossed molecular beam technique" not only pioneered new research fields in chemical dynamics but also made the world recognize Taiwan's scientific capabilities anew. From a child in Hsinchu to an internationally renowned scientist, Lee's life trajectory witnesses important milestones in Taiwan's scientific development.

## Early Life and Educational Background

### Hsinchu Years Enlightenment

Born November 19, 1936, in Hsinchu City, Hsinchu Prefecture (present-day Hsinchu City East District) during Japanese rule, Lee Yuan-tseh's family provided a stable growing environment amid that turbulent era. His father, Lee Tze-fan, was a renowned watercolor painter and elementary school principal; his mother, Tsai Pei, was a traditional homemaker devoted to family care. This family background combining art and education cultivated Lee's keen observation skills and intellectual curiosity.

**Childhood scientific enlightenment:**
Lee was naturally curious about natural phenomena from early age. He enjoyed observing insects, collecting specimens, and frequently dismantling household radios and clocks to understand their operating principles. This persistent pursuit of "why" became important motivation for his later dedication to scientific research.

**Wartime growth:**
During World War II, Lee spent his childhood and adolescence. Though war brought material shortages and social upheaval, it also cultivated his resilient character. He learned to find opportunities within difficulties and exercise creativity within limitations.

### Academic Foundation

**Hsinchu Middle School period:**
During his time at Hsinchu Middle School, Lee demonstrated excellent learning abilities. He particularly loved mathematics and physics, often discussing academic problems with classmates after school. During this period, he already showed strong interest in science, frequently reading scientific books in the library.

**National Taiwan University Chemistry Department:**
In 1955, Lee entered National Taiwan University's Chemistry Department. In that era when NTU was transitioning from the Japanese imperial university system, chemistry curriculum mainly focused on analytical and organic chemistry. However, Lee was more interested in the then-flourishing field of physical chemistry.

**Self-study spirit manifestation:**
Due to the department's lack of physical chemistry courses, Lee organized study groups with classmates during winter and summer breaks, self-studying emerging physical theories like thermodynamics and quantum mechanics. To read more international academic papers, he actively learned German, Russian, and other common academic languages.

**University research experience:**
During his time at NTU, Lee conducted research under chemistry professors. He participated in multiple chemical analysis experiments, cultivating rigorous experimental attitudes and keen observation skills. These early research experiences established solid foundations for his later scientific career.

### National Tsing Hua University Institute of Atomic Sciences

After graduating in 1959, Lee entered the Institute of Atomic Sciences at National Tsing Hua University in Hsinchu to pursue a master's degree. Here, he encountered more cutting-edge scientific research and began focusing on physical chemistry.

**Master's thesis research:**
Lee's master's thesis focused on atomic and molecular spectroscopy research. This work gave him deep understanding of relationships between molecular structures and their physical properties, planting seeds for his later Nobel Prize research.

**International perspective establishment:**
During his time at Tsing Hua, Lee read extensive foreign scientific literature, gradually building internationalized academic perspectives. He realized that achieving breakthroughs in scientific research required going beyond Taiwan to learn advanced techniques at world-class laboratories.

## Study in America: Career Turning Point

### University of California, Berkeley

In 1962, Lee received a scholarship to pursue doctoral studies at University of California, Berkeley. This decision changed his life and created history for Taiwan's scientific community.

**Doctoral research direction:**
At Berkeley, Lee chose chemical dynamics as his research focus. His doctoral thesis examined chemical reaction mechanisms, particularly energy transfer problems during molecular collision processes.

**Rigorous scientific training:**
Berkeley's academic environment was extremely demanding, requiring students to possess not only deep theoretical foundations but also exquisite experimental skills. Lee received world-class scientific training here, learning to design precise experiments, analyze complex data, and write high-quality academic papers.

### Harvard University Postdoctoral Research

In 1967, Lee went to Harvard University for postdoctoral research under Nobel Chemistry Prize winner Dudley R. Herschbach. This experience became the most crucial turning point in his scientific career.

**Birth of crossed molecular beam technique:**
At Harvard, Lee collaborated with Professor Herschbach to improve and develop crossed molecular beam technique. After two years of tireless work, they finally assembled the world's first crossed molecular beam instrument—a technology that revolutionarily changed chemical dynamics research methods.

**Breakthrough in technical innovation:**
Traditional chemical reaction studies were conducted under normal temperature and pressure, where gas molecules constantly collide with each other, changing directions and speeds, making molecular motion quite chaotic. Lee's crossed molecular beam technique could make two molecular beams collide at specific angles and velocities in high vacuum environments, precisely observing and analyzing chemical reaction processes.

**Research significance:**
This technology enabled scientists to "see" every step of chemical reactions at the molecular level, understanding how reactants transform into products, how energy transfers between molecules, and reaction stereochemistry details. This opened entirely new research fields in chemical dynamics.

## Academic Career Peak: Chicago and Berkeley Periods

### University of Chicago Professor Period

In 1969, Lee was appointed assistant professor in the Chemistry Department at University of Chicago, beginning his formal academic career in America. During five years in Chicago, he established his own laboratory, trained his first graduate students, and published many important research results.

**Laboratory establishment:**
Lee built an advanced molecular beam laboratory at University of Chicago. He not only guided student research but personally designed and improved experimental equipment. During this period, his technical innovation abilities were fully demonstrated.

### Return to Berkeley: Scientific Achievement Pinnacle

In 1974, Lee was invited to return to UC Berkeley as Chemistry Department professor. Here, his scientific research reached unprecedented heights.

**Continuous experimental technique improvement:**
Lee continuously improved crossed molecular beam technique, enabling study of more complex chemical reactions. He developed new detection methods capable of measuring reaction product internal energy distribution and angular distribution.

**Important research achievements:**

1. **Fluorine atom and hydrogen molecule reactions:**
Lee studied the seemingly simple F + H₂ → HF + H reaction in detail, discovering many unexpected phenomena. This research became a classic case in chemical dynamics textbooks.

2. **Oxygen atom and alkane reactions:**
He systematically studied oxygen atom reactions with various alkane molecules, discovering reaction stereoselectivity patterns, providing important theoretical foundations for combustion chemistry.

3. **Metal atom reaction dynamics:**
Lee pioneered studies of metal atom and molecule reactions, revealing molecular mechanisms of metal catalytic reactions.

## 1986 Nobel Prize in Chemistry: Taiwan's Pride

### Award Announcement

On October 15, 1986, the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences announced that year's Nobel Prize in Chemistry would be awarded to Dudley R. Herschbach, John Charles Polanyi, and Lee Yuan-tseh for their contributions to "research on chemical elementary process dynamics."

**Award rationale:**
The Nobel Committee specifically mentioned in their citation: "The work of these three scientists enables us to understand how chemical reactions proceed. Their research results hold fundamental importance for understanding and controlling chemical reactions."

**Taiwan's historic moment:**
Lee became the first Nobel Prize winner born and raised in Taiwan. This news caused tremendous sensation in Taiwan, with people throughout the island feeling pride and excitement for this historic achievement.

**International impact:**
Lee's Nobel Prize represented not only personal honor but made international society recognize Taiwan's scientific capabilities anew. This award proved Taiwan's ability to cultivate world-class scientific talent.

### Scientific Contribution Significance

**Technical innovation importance:**
Lee's crossed molecular beam technique was hailed as a revolutionary tool for chemical dynamics research. This technology enabled scientists to study chemical reactions at unprecedented detail levels, like using high-speed photography to film chemical reaction "movies."

**Theory and experiment integration:**
Lee's research not only provided precise experimental data but also promoted chemical dynamics theory development. His work bridged theoretical calculations and experimental observations.

**Application value:**
Crossed molecular beam technique applications span extremely wide ranges, from atmospheric chemistry to combustion science, from catalytic reactions to astrochemistry. This technology provided scientific foundations for solving many practical problems.

## Return to Taiwan Service: Academia Sinica President Period

### Decision to Return

In 1994, at his academic peak, Lee made a surprising decision: resign from UC Berkeley professorship and return to Taiwan as Academia Sinica president. This decision shocked the international academic community and excited Taiwan's scientific circles.

**Motivation for return:**
Lee stated in multiple interviews that he hoped to return home and contribute to Taiwan's scientific development. He believed Taiwan had potential to become Asia's scientific center, requiring more investment and construction.

**Homeland sentiment:**
Despite achieving great success in America, Lee always harbored deep affection for Taiwan. He hoped to use his experience and reputation to enhance Taiwan's scientific research standards.

### Academia Sinica President Term (1994-2006)

During twelve years as Academia Sinica president, Lee profoundly influenced Taiwan's scientific development.

**Organizational reform and modernization:**
Upon taking office, Lee immediately began Academia Sinica organizational reforms. He introduced internationalized management systems, established peer review mechanisms, and raised research quality standards.

**New institute establishments:**
Under his leadership, Academia Sinica established multiple new institutes, including Institute of Information Science, Institute of Biomedical Sciences, and Genomics Research Center, expanding research field breadth.

**International cooperation promotion:**
Lee actively promoted international cooperation, establishing partnerships with world-class research institutions. He invited many internationally renowned scholars to conduct research and exchange at Academia Sinica.

**Talent cultivation programs:**
He particularly emphasized young scientist cultivation, establishing multiple scholarships and research programs to encourage outstanding young people to dedicate themselves to scientific research.

## Continued Scientific Research and Innovation

### Research Direction Expansion

Even while serving in administrative roles, Lee maintained passion for scientific research. He expanded research focus to more fields:

**Environmental chemistry:**
Lee began concerning environmental issues, studying atmospheric pollution and ozone depletion environmental chemical processes. His team published multiple important papers on atmospheric chemical reaction mechanisms.

**Cluster chemistry:**
He pioneered metal cluster and molecular cluster research, which later developed into important nanotechnology branches.

**Surface science:**
Lee's research team also entered surface science fields, studying molecular adsorption and reaction processes on solid surfaces.

**Biological physical chemistry:**
During his Academia Sinica period, he also conducted biological molecule physical chemistry research, exploring molecular mechanisms in life processes.

## Social Participation and Public Service

### Education Reform Participation

Lee was not only an outstanding scientist but also an active promoter of education reform.

**Nine-year integrated curriculum participation:**
In late 1990s, Lee participated in Taiwan's nine-year integrated curriculum planning. He advocated curriculum design should cultivate student thinking abilities rather than cramming knowledge.

**Science education reform:**
He particularly concerned science education reform, believing students should learn science through experiments and observations rather than memorizing formulas and laws.

**Critical thinking cultivation:**
Lee emphasized education should cultivate students' critical thinking and creativity, teaching them to question, think, and innovate.

### Environmental Protection Advocacy

As a scientist, Lee deeply understood environmental problem severity and actively participated in environmental protection advocacy.

**Climate change research:**
His research team invested in climate change-related chemical process research, providing scientific basis for understanding global warming mechanisms.

**Environmental policy suggestions:**
Lee frequently provided environmental policy suggestions to government, including reducing carbon emissions, developing renewable energy, and protecting natural ecology.

**Public education:**
Through lectures and articles, he promoted environmental protection importance to the public, raising public environmental awareness.

## Academic Honors and International Recognition

### Major Academic Awards

Besides the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, Lee received numerous important academic awards:

**American Chemical Society Awards:**
- Harrison Howe Award (1977)
- Peter Debye Award (1981)
- Irving Langmuir Award (1981)

**International Awards:**
- Member, American Academy of Arts and Sciences (1975)
- Member, National Academy of Sciences (1979)
- Honorary Member, Japan Academy (2007)

**Honorary Doctoral Degrees:**
Lee received honorary doctoral degrees from multiple world-renowned universities, including University of Chicago, Waseda University, and Hong Kong University of Science and Technology.

## Contribution to Taiwan's Scientific Development

### Scientific Talent Cultivation

One of Lee's most important contributions to Taiwan's scientific development was cultivating numerous excellent scientific talents.

**Student achievements:**
Among his students and colleagues, many later became renowned scientists, university professors, and research institution leaders.

**Research culture establishment:**
Lee established rigorous scientific research culture in Taiwan, emphasizing realistic scientific spirit and internationalized academic standards.

**Cross-disciplinary cooperation:**
He promoted cooperation between different disciplines, fostering diversified development of Taiwan's scientific research.

### Scientific Infrastructure Construction

**Research equipment improvement:**
Under Lee's promotion, Taiwan's scientific research equipment was significantly upgraded, with many research institutions purchasing world-advanced experimental equipment.

**Synchrotron radiation facility:**
He actively promoted Taiwan's synchrotron radiation facility construction, which became an important scientific research platform for Taiwan.

**International cooperation networks:**
Lee helped Taiwan establish extensive international scientific cooperation networks, enhancing Taiwan's position in international scientific communities.

Lee Yuan-tseh's life story represents not only individual scientific achievement but also Taiwan's rise in international scientific communities. From a curious child in Hsinchu to a Nobel Prize winner, his journey demonstrates that with sufficient effort and persistence, anyone can achieve extraordinary accomplishments in science. His dedication to Taiwan's scientific development, particularly in talent cultivation and international cooperation, has laid solid foundations for Taiwan's continued scientific progress.
