# Taiwanese Literature During the Japanese Colonial Period

In the spring of 1895, Taiwan's literary destiny began a completely new chapter with the signing sounds of the Treaty of Shimonoseki. If Taiwanese literature during the Qing period was slowly nurtured in the womb of traditional Chinese culture, then the fifty years of the Japanese colonial period marked the painful yet brilliant birth of Taiwanese literature as an independent literary life under the intense impact of Western modernity.

This was the most complex, contradictory, and dramatic era in Taiwanese literary history. The colonizer's language became the tool for the colonized to express resistance; the new literary concepts brought by the oppressors inspired national awakening among the oppressed; the deepest cultural identity often emerged from the most intense cultural conflicts. During these fifty years, Taiwanese literature underwent multiple transformations—from classical to modern, from literary Chinese to vernacular, from Chinese to Japanese—and finally found its unique voice within multiple cultural tensions.

## The 1895 Cession: Literary Historical Rupture

In 1895, as the echoes of the First Sino-Japanese War's cannon fire faded, the signing of the Treaty of Shimonoseki pushed Taiwan onto another historical trajectory. For Taiwan's literati, this was not only political upheaval but a fundamental turning point in literary destiny.

### The Tragic Voice of Anti-Japanese Literature

"Spring sorrow is hard to dispel, forcing myself to view the mountains, / Past events alarm the heart, tears about to fall. / Four million people cry as one, / A year ago today Taiwan was ceded." This is poet Qiu Fengjia's (丘逢甲) lament in "Spring Sorrow" (春愁), expressing the grief and helplessness of Taiwanese intellectuals facing the fate of cession.

During the year of 1895, Taiwan saw numerous anti-Japanese literary works. These works, mainly poetry, expressed resistance to Japanese rule and longing for the motherland. Lian Yatang's (連雅堂) "Preface to the General History of Taiwan" (台灣通史序), though written much later, had spiritual roots tracing back to this period. The lament that "Taiwan truly has no history" reflected intellectuals' cultural consciousness to establish history and discourse for Taiwan.

However, more literati chose "internal crossing" (內渡), returning to mainland China. This massive cultural brain drain created a temporary rupture in Taiwan's literary development. The literati who remained in Taiwan either gradually fell silent under Japanese cultural policies or struggled painfully in conflicts between tradition and modernity, local and foreign influences.

## The New-Old Literature Debate: Baptism of Modernity

### Zhang Wojun: Pioneer of the New Literature Movement

In 1924, an article titled "A Letter to Taiwanese Youth" was published in the *Taiwan Minpao* (台灣民報), written by Zhang Wojun (張我軍) who had just returned from Beijing. This article fiercely criticized Taiwan's traditional poetry and prose as "decadent and unbearable," advocating learning from the new literature promoted by Hu Shi and others, creating works in vernacular Chinese.

Zhang Wojun's views sparked intense debate. Traditional literati led by Lian Yatang firmly opposed, believing vernacular Chinese was "shallow and tasteless," damaging the depth of Chinese culture. The new literature faction led by Zhang Wojun argued that only vernacular Chinese could express modern people's thoughts and feelings and make literature truly reach the masses.

The significance of this debate far exceeded literature itself. It was actually a fundamental discussion about Taiwan's cultural development direction: Should Taiwan maintain tradition or embrace modernity? Should it preserve elite culture or move toward popular culture? Should it insist on "national language" (Chinese) or accept the new "national language" (Japanese)?

### *Taiwan Minpao*: Cradle of New Literature

Founded in 1925, the *Taiwan Minpao* became an important base for the new literature movement. This newspaper not only published political news but, more importantly, provided a publishing platform for Taiwan's new literature. Many important writers' debut works were published in this newspaper.

The literary pages of *Taiwan Minpao* showed clear characteristics of the era: on one hand, publishing new poems, novels, and essays in vernacular Chinese; on the other, preserving sections for traditional poetry, reflecting the transitional feature of old and new coexisting. More importantly, this newspaper began focusing on Taiwan's local social reality, laying the foundation for the rise of realist literature.

## Lai He: Father of Taiwan's New Literature

If we were to choose the most important Taiwanese writer of the Japanese colonial period, Lai He (賴和) (1894-1943) would undoubtedly be the choice. This country doctor from Changhua laid the foundation for Taiwan's new literature with his literary works and is revered as the "Father of Taiwan's New Literature."

### Literary Enlightenment and National Awakening

Lai He's literary creation was inseparable from his political awakening. In 1921, he participated in activities of the Taiwan Cultural Association and began focusing on Taiwan's social problems. In 1925, he published the essay "Untitled" (無題) and the new poem "Sacrifice Under Awakening—To Comrades of the Erllin Incident" (覺悟下的犧牲——寄二林事件的戰友), formally entering new literature creation.

Lai He's representative work "Fighting for Excitement" (鬥鬧熱) (1926) is considered Taiwan's first truly vernacular novel. This novel, set against temple fair background, depicted Taiwanese people's daily life while cleverly interspersing criticism of Japanese rule. The novel's language was vivid and lively, extensively using Taiwanese vocabulary, pioneering Taiwan literature's localization.

### Foundation of Realism

Lai He's literary creation consistently focused on social reality, particularly the living difficulties of ordinary people. His novel "A Scale" (一桿「稱仔」) depicted vendor Qin Desheng's unjust treatment due to weights and measures reform, profoundly revealing the absurdity and cruelty of colonial rule.

This realist writing tendency established an important tradition for Taiwanese literature. It was not art for art's sake but literature's intervention in reality, reflecting people's hardships and enlightening public wisdom. This tradition later influenced numerous writers like Yang Kui (楊逵) and Lu Heruo (呂赫若), becoming an important characteristic of Taiwanese literature.

### Language Experimentation and Innovation

Lai He's language experimentation had pioneering significance. His works extensively used Taiwanese vocabulary and expressions, sometimes directly using Chinese characters for Taiwanese phonetic transcription. This attempt caused considerable controversy at the time but established the direction of Taiwanese literature's linguistic localization.

More importantly, through language experimentation, Lai He found appropriate ways to express Taiwanese experience. His literary language maintained Chinese literary quality while incorporating the liveliness of Taiwanese speech, creating a modern Chinese with local characteristics.

## Rise of Leftist Literature

### Yang Kui: Standard-bearer of Proletarian Literature

Yang Kui (楊逵) (1906-1985) was an important representative of Taiwanese leftist literature. He studied in Japan in his early years, encountered Marxist thought and proletarian literary theory, and after returning to Taiwan, devoted himself to creating and promoting socialist literature.

Yang Kui's representative work "The Newspaper Boy" (送報伸) was Taiwan's first novel featuring a worker as protagonist. This work depicted a newspaper delivery boy's tragic encounters, profoundly revealing capitalist society's injustice. The novel's language was plain and powerful with vivid character images, embodying proletarian literature's aesthetic characteristics.

In 1935, Yang Kui founded *Taiwan New Literature* (台灣新文學) magazine, Taiwan's first purely literary magazine. The magazine not only published works by local writers but also extensively translated foreign leftist literary works, providing international perspective for Taiwanese literature.

### Zhang Shenqie and *Taiwan Literary Arts*

In 1934, *Taiwan Literary Arts* (台灣文藝) magazine was founded. Founder Zhang Shenqie (張深切) proposed the editorial policy of "regardless of doctrine, regardless of faction," broadly uniting Taiwan's literary forces. This magazine became one of the most influential literary publications in Taiwanese literary history.

The characteristic of *Taiwan Literary Arts* was inclusivity and diversity. It published both leftist writers' works and modernist creations; both Chinese-language writings and Japanese works; it focused on Taiwan while also looking toward world literature. This open attitude laid the foundation for Taiwan literature's diverse development.

## Literary Struggle During the Imperial Subject Period

### Rise of Japanese-Language Literature

After the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War in 1937, Japan implemented the "Imperial Subject Movement" (皇民化運動) in Taiwan, banning public use of Chinese and forcibly promoting Japanese education. Against this background, Taiwan saw a group of local writers creating in Japanese.

These writers faced extremely complex cultural circumstances. On one hand, they had to create using the colonizer's language; on the other, they hoped to maintain concern for Taiwan's local culture in their works. This contradiction produced unique literary tension and created some excellent works.

### Lu Heruo: Artist Transcending Language

Lu Heruo (呂赫若) (1914-1951) was the most important writer of this period. His novel "Ox Cart" (牛車) depicted Taiwan's rural decline with meticulous brushwork, showing farmers' helplessness and struggle under modernization's impact. Though written in Japanese, the work's spiritual core remained deeply rooted in Taiwan's soil.

Lu Heruo's creation embodied colonial writers' cultural strategy: superficially cooperating with colonial policies while actually expressing protection of local culture through literary metaphor and symbolism. This "indirect writing" approach became an important tradition in later Taiwanese literature.

### Zhang Wenhuan: Literature's Perseverer

Zhang Wenhuan (張文環) (1909-1978) was another important Japanese-language literary writer. His novel "The Castrated Rooster" (閹雞) used a story of a rooster's castration to metaphorically represent Taiwanese people's fate under colonial rule. This symbolic technique both avoided political censorship and expressed profound political allegory.

In 1941, Zhang Wenhuan founded *Taiwan Literature* (台灣文學) magazine, the most important literary publication during the Imperial Subject period. Though the magazine used Japanese, it still maintained Taiwan's local literary stance, providing precious publishing opportunities for Taiwanese writers.

## Aesthetic Qualities of Colonial Literature

### Double Consciousness in Literary Expression

Taiwanese literature during the Japanese period had obvious characteristics of "double consciousness." Writers had to face the reality of colonial rule while maintaining identification with local culture; they had to learn modern literary techniques while insisting on national literature's stance. This complex cultural situation produced unique literary aesthetics.

This double consciousness manifested at many levels: language choice (Chinese or Japanese), theme selection (reality or tradition), stance handling (resistance or cooperation), style determination (local or international). It was precisely in these complex choices that Taiwanese literature gradually formed its own characteristics.

### Deepening of Realism

Taiwanese literature during the Japanese period was dominated by realism. This realism was not simple life description but carried strong social critical color and national consciousness. Through literary works, writers exposed colonial rule's injustice, reflected people's suffering, and expressed pursuit of social justice.

This realist literature's characteristic was closely combining individual destiny with national destiny. A farmer's bankruptcy often implied the entire agricultural society's decline; an intellectual's struggle often reflected the entire nation's predicament. This combination of grand narrative and personal narrative provided rich expressive space for Taiwanese literature.

### Localized Literary Language

Despite experiencing multiple language transitions, Taiwanese literature during the Japanese period consistently sought literary language suitable for expressing Taiwanese experience. Whether Lai He's Taiwanese literary Chinese, Yang Kui's popular Chinese, or Lu Heruo's "Taiwan-style Japanese," all embodied writers' persistent exploration of literary language localization.

The significance of this exploration lay not only in finding tools of expression but also in establishing Taiwanese literature's linguistic characteristics. What should Taiwanese literature's language be like? How to embody local characteristics while maintaining literariness? These explorations provided valuable experience for post-war Taiwanese literature development.

## Historical Turning Point

On August 15, 1945, with the Japanese Emperor's surrender announcement, Taiwan's Japanese colonial era officially ended. For Taiwanese literature, this was both one era's end and another era's beginning.

The fifty years of the Japanese period left rich legacy for Taiwanese literature: establishment of modern literary concepts, awakening of localization consciousness, tolerance of multicultural diversity, foundation of realist tradition. These all became important bases for post-war Taiwanese literature development.

Simultaneously, this period also exposed some problems in Taiwanese literature's development: confusion over language identity, ambiguity of cultural identity, complexity of political stance. These problems persisted after the war and appeared in new forms under different historical conditions.

## Conclusion: Blooming in the Cracks

Taiwanese literature during the Japanese period was a flower blooming in the cracks. It was caught between tradition and modernity, between local and foreign, between resistance and cooperation. It was precisely within these complex tensions that Taiwanese literature found its voice and established its character.

Lai He's physician's pen, Yang Kui's fighting voice, Lu Heruo's artistic beauty, Zhang Wenhuan's persistent will—these different literary aspects together constituted the rich panorama of Taiwanese literature during the Japanese period. They proved through their creations that even under the most difficult historical conditions, literature could still persist in its ideals and express the nation's voice.

Taiwanese literature of this period laid a solid foundation for later development. Its realist tradition, localization consciousness, resistance spirit, and cultural tolerance all became important characteristics of Taiwanese literature that influence to this day. As Lai He wrote in his poem: "Warriors should fight for justice"—this literary courage and ideal remain Taiwanese literature's most precious treasure today.
