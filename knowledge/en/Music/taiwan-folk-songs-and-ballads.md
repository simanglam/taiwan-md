# Taiwan Folk Songs and Ballads: The Island’s Deepest Memory

Taiwan’s folk songs are the island’s most intimate archive. Before the age of recording, they carried history, grief, humor, and desire from one generation to the next. They are not just music; they are living memory—voices tied to land, labor, migration, and survival. Across centuries, Taiwan’s folk repertoire has absorbed Indigenous polyphony, Hoklo (臺語) street songs, and Hakka mountain melodies, while responding to colonial rule, martial law, and the cultural revival of the late 20th century.

## Indigenous Music: The Oldest Layer of Sound

### Mountains, Sea, and Sacred Voice

Indigenous music is Taiwan’s oldest soundscape. For many Indigenous nations, songs are not separate from life—they are the way people communicate with ancestors, nature, and community. The melodies are often circular and trance‑like; the rhythms echo waves, wind, and footsteps. This is music as ritual, not entertainment.

### Difang Tuwana (郭英男): A Voice Heard Worldwide

Amis elder **Difang Tuwana (郭英男, 1921–2002)** was a cultural guardian whose singing introduced Taiwan’s Indigenous music to the world. His voice reached global audiences in the 1990s when “world music” became a major cultural movement. Unlike pop recordings, his songs did not need modern arrangements—the raw timbre and spiritual intensity were enough. He became a symbol of Indigenous cultural continuity, carrying the memory of the Malan community in Taitung.

### The Bunun Eight‑Part Harmony (Pasibutbut)

The **Bunun “Pasibutbut”**—often described as “eight‑part harmony”—is one of the world’s most famous Indigenous choral traditions. It is not a Western eight‑voice structure; it is a living, gradual build of harmonies that imitates the natural world. The singers align by intuition, not sheet music. When this music traveled to Europe in the late 1980s, audiences described it as celestial. UNESCO’s recognition of its cultural value is not only about musical complexity, but about the survival of an ancient social practice.

Indigenous songs remain functional today: prayer, harvest, healing, and community memory. Modern Indigenous artists (such as A‑Mei, Shunza, and other singers rooted in tribal heritage) carry these traditions forward in contemporary forms.

## Hoklo (臺語) Songs: The Golden Age of the Japanese Era

### “Four Seasons of Longing”: The Birth of Taiwanese Pop

In the 1930s, Taiwan experienced a cultural awakening under Japanese rule. Composer **Deng Yu‑xian (鄧雨賢)** created a set of four iconic songs—**“Wang Chun‑feng (望春風),” “Rainy Night Flower (雨夜花),” “Moonlit Sorrow (月夜愁),”** and **“Four Seasons Red (四季紅)”**—collectively known as **“Four Seasons of Longing” (四月望雨)**. These songs fused Taiwanese language with Western harmony and Japanese sentimental style, producing the first Taiwanese pop classics.

The themes were universal—love, longing, seasonal change—but the language made them local. For many Taiwanese, these songs still evoke a sense of nostalgia and tenderness, like a collective memory stored in melody.

### The Female Voices of Early Taiwanese Pop

Singer **Chun‑chun (純純, Liu Qing‑xiang)** became the voice of this era. A stage performer from the opera world, her recordings of “Wang Chun‑feng” and “Dancing Era (跳舞時代)” made her a star. Yet her life also reveals the gendered stigma of the time: female singers were often regarded as “entertainers” rather than artists. She died young, and her story is inseparable from the bittersweet tone of the songs she sang.

### Cultural Suppression Under Japanization

After 1937, the Japanese government intensified **kominka** (皇民化) policies. Taiwanese songs were rewritten into Japanese‑language propaganda; “Wang Chun‑feng” was transformed into a song encouraging colonial settlement. Yet in private spaces, Taiwanese people kept singing the original lyrics—a quiet form of resistance and cultural memory.

## Hakka Mountain Songs: Fieldwork as Poetry

Hakka music forms another major thread in Taiwan’s folk repertoire. The tradition of **shan’ge (山歌)**—mountain songs—was sung during tea picking and farm work. It is often improvised, sometimes playful, sometimes romantic. The Hakka phrase “nine tunes, eighteen styles (九腔十八調)” captures how varied the melodic patterns are across regions and dialects.

Unlike staged folk music, Hakka mountain songs were social encounters: one person sang, another responded. The songs were a way to flirt, to tease, or to express hardship. Contemporary Hakka artists have kept this spirit alive, reshaping it for modern audiences while honoring its roots.

## The Post‑War Silence and Revival

### Language Policy and Cultural Eclipse

After 1945, Taiwan’s government imposed a strict Mandarin‑only policy. Taiwanese, Hakka, and Indigenous languages were pushed out of education and media. Folk songs in mother tongues were marginalized; many singers switched to Mandarin or left the industry. Cultural memory did not vanish, but it was silenced.

### The Persistence of Folk Voices

In the 1950s and 1960s, some artists continued to sing in Taiwanese, despite limited broadcasting. Their songs often centered on homeland, loss, and nostalgia—emotions shaped by political suppression. These works kept the folk tradition alive during a period of cultural drought.

### The Post‑Martial‑Law Renaissance

Taiwan lifted martial law in 1987, and a wave of cultural revival followed. Artists like **Chen Ming‑chang (陳明章)**, **Wu Bai (伍佰)**, and **Lim Giong (林強)** built modern Taiwanese songs that carried folk sensibilities into rock, blues, and electronic music. The folk tradition became a foundation for new musical identities.

At the same time, government and civic groups launched festivals, folk contests, and reissue projects, bringing classic Taiwanese songs back into public consciousness and giving younger generations a way to reconnect with their own cultural roots.

## Why These Songs Still Matter

Taiwan’s folk songs are not simply historical artifacts. They are emotional technology—ways to remember, to mourn, to celebrate, and to resist. Each song is a tiny archive of lived experience, showing how ordinary people navigated colonization, modernization, and cultural pressure.

In a globalized world, the question is no longer whether folk songs can survive; it is how they will evolve. Many of today’s musicians are blending Indigenous chants with electronic music, or rewriting Hakka melodies into indie rock. This is not dilution; it is how living traditions adapt.

## Closing Note: Listening to the Island

To listen to Taiwan’s folk songs is to hear the island’s heartbeat. These songs hold the emotional DNA of Taiwan—its grief, tenderness, resilience, and humor. They remain a bridge between past and present, giving voice to the people whose stories might otherwise fade into silence.

As long as these melodies continue to be sung—whether in villages, on stages, or through streaming platforms—Taiwan’s cultural memory remains alive.
