# Taiwan: The Fruit Kingdom

> **30-Second Overview:** Taiwan's mango industry is worth approximately NT$7.6 billion annually, with every two mangoes nationally, one comes from Tainan. But a political storm in 2021 caused custard apples (shìjiā 釋迦), 90% dependent on the Chinese market, to suddenly lose their export outlet. Behind this lies Taiwan's fruit industry's real predicament: climate change threats, aging labor force, and over-concentrated export risks.

In the scorching summer heat, a single Irwin mango from Yujing can sell for over NT$100 per *catty* (600g). At this price, you could eat breakfast for three days. But fruit farmers say: "We don't want to sell this expensive—Mother Nature just won't cooperate."

In May 2025, consecutive extreme weather events severely reduced Taiwan's mango production. Simultaneously, custard apple farmers who previously depended on the Chinese market were still struggling with the sudden 2021 import ban. On one side, natural disasters; on the other, man-made crises. Taiwan, known as the "Fruit Kingdom," faces unprecedented challenges.

## The Truth Behind Yujing's Mango Legend

When thinking of Taiwan mangoes, Yujing comes to mind first. Roughly every two mangoes in Taiwan come from Tainan, and Yujing serves as the capital of this mango empire.

But Yujing's mango dominance wasn't built overnight. Irwin mangoes were originally introduced in 1954 by Kaohsiung County Farmers' Association from Florida, USA. After years of cultivation, they found their ideal growing environment in Tainan's mountainous areas. Yujing's elevation of 200-300 meters, significant day-night temperature variations, and well-draining red soil created Irwin mangoes' distinctive sweet fragrance.

The numbers speak: Taiwan's mango cultivation covers approximately 15,000 hectares, with annual production of 153,000 tons worth about NT$7.6 billion. Tainan City alone accounts for half of national production, with Yujing District as Tainan's core mango region.

Behind the glory lies reality's challenges. Elderly Yujing farmer Chen says: "Now a single Irwin mango can sell for over NT$100, but this isn't good news. Prices are this high because production dropped nearly half."

Climate change is the greatest enemy. 2025 spring's abnormal high temperatures disrupted mango flowering periods; May's consecutive heavy rains damaged fruit quality just before harvest. Farmers' investment costs haven't decreased—labor, fertilizers, and pesticides all increased in price, but yields become increasingly unstable.

## The Custard Apple's Chinese Dream Shattered

If mangoes face natural disasters, custard apples face man-made ones.

In 2003, Taiwan began exporting custard apples targeting the Chinese market. After over a decade of cultivation, China accounted for 99.4% of Taiwan's total custard apple exports by 2016. Taitung farmers called this complete reliance on a single market "putting all eggs in one basket."

On September 20, 2021, China suddenly banned fresh Taiwanese custard apple imports, citing "pest detection." Overnight, 90% of export-dependent custard apples lost their market. During peak season, farmers watched their carefully grown "golden fruit" rot on trees.

The Council of Agriculture urgently announced plans to redirect 5,000 tons of custard apples to Japan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, and other markets. But reality was harsh: custard apples can't tolerate low temperatures, requiring 7-10 days for sea transport, often arriving softened and spoiled in Japan. Moreover, Japanese consumers prefer "elegant eating" fruits and showed low acceptance for seed-spitting custard apples.

Taitung Custard Apple Farmers' Association director says: "Custard apples sell for 1,000 yen (about NT$300) each in Japan, but the market is too small to absorb our production volume."

## Labor Crisis: No Young People Want to Farm

Beyond natural disasters and political risks, Taiwan fruit farmers face another reality: nobody wants to inherit the farms.

Walking into any orchard, you'll see gray-haired elderly farmers. According to Ministry of Agriculture statistics, Taiwan farmers average over 62 years old—better than Japan's 67 years but concerning nonetheless.

Why don't young people want to farm? Second-generation Yujing farmer Xiao Lin says: "After university graduation, I worked in Taipei earning NT$50,000 monthly. Returning to grow mangoes, earning NT$1 million annually is impressive, but that million comes from 365 days without vacation. We rush harvest before typhoons, spray pesticides for pests—where are weekends?"

Labor shortages directly increase costs. Workers who cost NT$200 daily previously now cost NT$300-400 and aren't always available. During harvest season, farmers must import foreign workers from Vietnam and Indonesia.

Mechanization offers partial solutions, but Taiwan's orchards are mostly on hillsides with rugged terrain unsuitable for large machinery. Government-promoted "smart agriculture" sounds wonderful, but for small farmers, an automated irrigation system costing NT$500,000 is unaffordable.

## Export Dilemma: All Eggs in One Basket

Taiwan fruit export difficulties aren't limited to custard apples. Over the past decade, China has been Taiwan's most important fresh fruit export market, with annual purchases ranging from NT$1-4 billion. Besides custard apples, pineapples and wax apples also heavily depend on the Chinese market.

This over-concentration risk exploded in 2021. In March, China banned Taiwan pineapples; in September, custard apples and wax apples. The reasons were all "pest detection," but industry insiders knew this was political manipulation.

The government announced market "diversification," but easier said than done. Japan offers high prices but strict quarantine standards and appearance requirements; Southeast Asian markets have limited purchasing power and their own tropical fruit production bases; European and American markets involve distant transportation and high costs.

For Taiwan fruits to truly "go global" requires not just government policy support but industry-wide upgrading: quality standardization, brand marketing, cold-chain logistics—each requiring massive investment.

## Climate Change: Long-term Threat

If political risks are short-term shocks, climate change is a long-term threat.

Ministry of Agriculture reports indicate rising temperatures may shift Taiwan's fruit cultivation zones, advance or delay fruiting periods, and reduce quality. More frightening is increased extreme weather: super typhoons, super heavy rainfall, record-breaking heat—all causing devastating damage to fruit trees.

Legislative Yuan analysis shows natural disaster agricultural losses averaged NT$12.4 billion annually over the past 15 years, reaching NT$38.34 billion in 2016. Behind these figures lie countless farmers' blood, sweat, and tears.

Traditional disaster compensation can no longer address such challenges. The government promotes agricultural insurance, but high premiums and strict claim conditions discourage small farmer participation. Many farmers prefer gambling on luck rather than paying insurance premiums.

## Breaking Through: Quality and Innovation

Facing multiple difficulties, Taiwan fruit farmers haven't given up.

In quality improvement, more farmers adopt "premium routes." Taitung custard apple farmers switching to organic cultivation sell individual fruits for NT$300; Pingtung wax apple farmers develop "Black Pearl" varieties—purple-black skin with extremely high sweetness—becoming market darlings.

In marketing innovation, many farmers livestream fruit sales, bypassing middlemen to directly face consumers. During the pandemic, online home delivery became trendy, with packaging-savvy farmers earning more than before.

In technological innovation, facility agriculture spreads. Despite large initial investments, environmental factors become controllable, reducing natural disaster risks. Some large orchards introduce greenhouse cultivation, automatic irrigation, and pest monitoring systems.

## The Fruit Kingdom at a Crossroads

Taiwan's fruit industry now stands at a crossroads. Going left might mean wallowing in past glory, continuing reliance on traditional production methods and single markets; going right requires facing transformation's pain and uncertainty.

Government roles matter but aren't omnipotent. Industrial policies need long-term planning, not sudden turns due to short-term political storms. Farmers need stable policy environments for confident investment and long-term planning.

Consumer support is also crucial. Taiwan fruits are high-quality but relatively expensive. If consumers willingly pay reasonable prices for quality, they can support farmers' continuous improvement.

Most importantly, Taiwan's fruit industry must shift from "quantity" competition to "quality" competition. Instead of competing on volume with Southeast Asian countries, focus on quality, safety, and branding. Taiwan's fruit technology and quality control capabilities remain world-class.

## More Than Sweetness: Resilience

Today, when we bite into sweet Taiwan fruit, we taste not just the fruit's sweetness but farmers' resilience. Under climate change threats, market competition pressure, and labor shortage difficulties, Taiwan fruit farmers persist in growing world-class fruits.

Taiwan's "Fruit Kingdom" title isn't for largest production but highest quality. Behind this title lies generations of farmers' persistence and innovation. As long as this persistence remains, Taiwan fruits will continue shining on the world stage.
